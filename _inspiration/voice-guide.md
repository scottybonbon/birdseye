# The Birdseye Voice

Or: how to write copy that operators trust.

This is the discipline doc for every line on the site. The-one-rule.md Section 4 names the principle; this file shows you how to write to it. When in doubt about a piece of copy, this file is the reference.

---

## The principle

**Every line on this site is one yard ops director talking to another.** If it doesn't sound like that, rewrite it. Marketing language is what convinces people who already wanted to be convinced. Operator language is what convinces people who didn't.

The voice we're chasing is somewhere between Apple's confidence, Stripe's clarity, Linear's brevity, and the matter-of-fact register of a senior operator briefing a new hire on how the yard actually runs. None of those four sources sound like a SaaS landing page. We don't either.

---

## Who we're talking to

Four people. The tone holds across all of them; the angle shifts:

**Yard ops director** — what they read first. Cares about throughput, dwell time, gate processing, shift logistics. Speaks operator. Distrusts marketing language. Will close the tab the moment they see "streamline."

**Security director** — perimeter, intervention, response time, audit-grade evidence. Wants intervention paths and chain-of-custody specifics. Has been pitched by every camera vendor in the last five years; needs to know we're different.

**Compliance officer** — record integrity, retention, claims defense, certifications. Wants specifics, not vibes. Will skim for SOC 2 / ISO 27001 / FSMA references and judge the page on whether they exist.

**CFO / VP-Finance** — cost story, ROI, margin recapture. Wants confident numbers with cited methodology. Will reject any percentage that doesn't carry a footnote.

The voice is the same for all four — confident, declarative, operator-grade. The *angle* shifts depending on the section.

## Five voice attributes

### 1. Declarative. Statements, not pitches.

The default mode is "this is what's true." Not "we believe," not "we help," not "we enable." Birdseye runs the yard. Birdseye verifies the BOL. Birdseye seals the record.

- ✗ *"Birdseye helps yard operators streamline their gate operations and improve throughput."*
- ✓ *"Birdseye runs the gate."*

### 2. Specific. Real nouns, real numbers, real verbs.

Generic adjectives are filler. Specific nouns are evidence. "75% faster gate processing" beats "significantly faster." "Sub-2-second handoff" beats "near-instant." When you can't be specific, cut the line.

- ✗ *"Significantly faster gate processing."*
- ✓ *"Gate dwell down 75% on average."*

### 3. Confident, not boastful. Cite the work; let the work speak.

We're not the leading-best-cutting-edge anything. We're the system that does X. Awards-tier copy never tells you it's awards-tier — the work tells you. The same posture applies to product copy: name what we do, name what we measure, let the reader infer the rank.

- ✗ *"We're the leading provider of AI-powered yard automation."*
- ✓ *"Most customers cut security spend 25–50%."*

### 4. Witty when earned. Never for its own sake. Always serving clarity.

Wordplay that makes a sentence sharper is a gift. Wordplay that makes you slow down to admire it is a tax. Birdseye's best lines are witty *and* faster to read than the alternative — that's the bar.

- ✓ *"Cameras see. Sirens scare. People talk."* (three-word verbs ladder into the brand thesis — wit IS the clarity)
- ✓ *"Six lines that don't appear on anyone else's site."* (concrete claim packaged as confidence)
- ✗ *"At Birdseye, we're 'fore-seeing' the future of yard ops."* (pun for pun's sake — actively slows down)

### 5. Operator-shorthand-aware. Use the words yard people use.

Use the acronyms operators use. BOL not Bill of Lading (or both, which signals you know the audience). Spotter not yard worker. Dwell not waiting time. The vocabulary itself signals "we know your operation." Refusing the shorthand reads as outsider.

## What we never write

These words and phrases are banned. If they show up, the line gets rewritten:

- "Streamline," "transform," "unlock," "elevate," "empower," "enable"
- "Discover the future of," "the next generation of," "reimagine"
- "Cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "world-leading," "industry-leading," "premier"
- "Solutions" (we run a system, not "solutions")
- "Innovation" as a noun ("our innovation," "the innovation we deliver")
- "Seamless," "seamlessly," "frictionless"
- "Leverage" as a verb
- "Holistic," "comprehensive," "robust," "scalable" (without specifics)
- Any sentence that begins "We believe," "We think," "We're committed to"
- "It's important to note that"
- "In today's fast-paced [anything]"

The list looks long but it's the same five sins repeated: hedging, hyperbole, abstraction, apology, fluff.

## Per-surface rules

### Eyebrows (mono-caps, 2-4 words)

Section name + qualifier in the filing register. The eyebrow tells the reader what kind of moment this is.

- ✓ `FAQ · COMMON QUESTIONS`
- ✓ `ARCHIVE · GATECORE DETECTION · 2024`
- ✓ `THE METHOD · MAXIMUM TELEPRESENCE™`
- ✗ `EXCITING NEW FEATURE` (marketing register, not filing register)

### Headlines (one or two clauses)

Three-beat declarative when possible. One italic word per H1 — the italic carries the load-bearing emphasis. Specific verbs (verifies, seals, catches, holds, runs). Never abstract verbs (delivers, provides, helps, enables).

- ✓ *"Smarter yards. Safer business. Zero guesswork."*
- ✓ *"Cameras see. Sirens scare. People talk."*
- ✓ *"Stop fictitious pickups at the gate."*
- ✗ *"Helping operators streamline their workflows."*

### Subheads / body

One claim per sentence. Numbers cite themselves (footnoted to methodology in the metric strip's `note` field). Active voice. Subject + verb + object. Cut every adverb that isn't load-bearing.

A second sentence is allowed only if it's doing different work than the first. If it's just restating the headline more politely, cut it.

### CTAs

Operator-specific. Action-first verb. Never *"Learn more"* — always specify what.

- ✓ *"See it on your yard"* (operator-specific)
- ✓ *"Run the ROI"* (action verb + object)
- ✓ *"How GateCore prevents it"* (specific outcome)
- ✗ *"Learn more"* (vague)
- ✗ *"Click here"* (vague + UI literal)
- ✗ *"Get started"* (vague + generic)

The exception: *"Book a demo"* is the canonical primary CTA across the site. It's industry shorthand; operators recognize it; it stays.

### Pull quotes

Customer voice as architecture. Always with attribution and filing date. Never fabricated. Anonymization integrity per the rule book — italic-serif role + region for anonymized; sans bold name + role for named.

## Examples that nailed it

> *"Smarter yards. Safer business. Zero guesswork."*
> Three beats, parallel structure, statement → promise → antithesis. The hero. Don't touch.

> *"Cameras see. Sirens scare. People talk."*
> Three-word verbs that ladder into the brand thesis. Wit AS clarity, not wit for its own sake.

> *"AI catches it. A live agent decides. The system writes it down."*
> Three actors, three actions, no adjectives. Maximum Telepresence in 13 words.

> *"Strategic theft tripled. The front door is the new attack surface."*
> Stat + reframe. The article you've already read in your industry magazine, condensed.

> *"Presence isn't pixels. It's the discipline of being there."*
> Defines the brand idea as a posture, not a feature. The sentence the founder says when nobody's pitching.

> *"Six lines that don't appear on anyone else's site."*
> Tells you exactly what's coming. Confident without boasting. Lets the next six items do the work.

> *"Build with us. Sell with us. Show up with us."*
> Three-beat partnership invitation. The "show up" is the operator-grade verb that turns a generic invitation into a relationship document.

## How to use this

Before writing any new copy: read the relevant per-surface rule.

Before publishing any new copy: read it aloud. If it sounds like an operator briefing, ship it. If it sounds like a SaaS landing page, rewrite it.

When in doubt: cut a word. Then cut another. Stop when the meaning changes; the line is now as short as it needs to be.

The five protected lines above (Hero, Maximum Telepresence subhead, Voice-Down premise, Cargo Theft headline, Certainty closer, Six Lines) don't get edited away. They're the gold standard. New work is judged against them.
